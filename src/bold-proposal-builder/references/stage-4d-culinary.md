# Stage 4d — Culinary Reference

## Purpose

Food is the fastest emotional channel at any event. It is what guests remember when they forget the speeches. Stage 4d authors a menu that is tied to the brand heart, covers the dietary spectrum of the audience, and matches the service style to the event's flow and tone.

Output: `04-specialists/culinary/menu.md`

---

## The four decisions

Every culinary plan rests on four choices. Make them deliberately.

### 1. Service style
- **Seated (מוגש / plated)**, formal, controlled timing, high staff ratio. Feels ceremonial. Works for 40 to 400 guests.
- **Stations (עמדות)**, active, social, guests move. Feels contemporary. Works for 100 to 800 guests.
- **Passed / roaming (מוגש על מגש)**, elegant, low-commitment, keeps guests standing. Works for cocktail hours and short events.
- **Family-style / shared platters (משפחתי)**, warm, intimate, encourages conversation across the table. Works for 30 to 200 guests.
- **Hybrid**, e.g., passed hors d'oeuvres during arrival, then seated dinner. Common and usually good.

**Rule:** match service style to event tone. A minimal, quiet event does not want frantic stations. A celebratory crowd does not want formal plated service that slows them down.

### 2. Dietary coverage

Default coverage for Israeli events (adjust per audience):
- **Vegetarian / vegan:** always offered. Usually 20 to 35% of guests in Israel opt for it.
- **Kosher:** depends on audience and venue. If uncertain, design kosher-compliant as the default unless the client explicitly wants otherwise.
- **Gluten-free:** always have at least 2 to 3 clearly marked options.
- **Allergens:** nut-free signage, shellfish declared.
- **Halal:** for mixed audiences, ensure at least a clear path.
- **Alcohol:** ask explicitly. In some audiences (religious, family events with minors, corporate during working hours) a non-alcoholic bar is the right default, not an afterthought.

**Pescatarian note:** Hemi is pescatarian. When the client is Bold itself or a Hemi-adjacent event, pescatarian options are a personal priority. Mark them prominently.

### 3. Menu narrative

A menu is not a list. It is a short story. It has a beginning (opening, light, wakes up the palate), a middle (the substantive center, generous), and an end (close, sweet or bitter, memorable).

Each dish should tie to the event's concept or motif in one of these ways:
- **Material echo**, the same material family the motif uses (e.g., brushed brass motif → copper-glazed plates; concrete motif → stoneware).
- **Palette echo**, dish colors that live in the brand palette.
- **Geometric echo**, a recurring shape appears in plating.
- **Conceptual echo**, the dish's idea references the concept (e.g., a "new classic" concept = familiar dishes with one unexpected element).

Avoid literalism: the concept is "fire" does not mean everything must be grilled. Subtle echoes are stronger than obvious ones.

### 4. Beverage pairing

Match drinks to dishes, atmosphere, and hour. Three levels to consider:
- **Arrival drink**, one signature drink that captures the event's opening feel. Non-alcoholic version must be equally considered, not an afterthought.
- **Dinner pairing**, wine or cocktail per course; or a single well-chosen house wine for simplicity.
- **Late-night / post-dinner**, the bar that runs as the evening loosens.

## Presentation details that matter

- **Plates and glasses are part of the design.** A beautiful menu on white IKEA plates misses half the effect. Note the tableware choice in the motif section.
- **Table setting tied to motif.** If the motif is "a single horizontal line", the table runner should reinforce it. If the motif is "brushed brass + concrete", the cutlery should echo it.
- **Labels and cards.** Printed food cards at stations are a low-cost amplifier of the brand heart. Mention them.
- **Garnishes and details are visible.** Guests photograph food. Design it to be photographed (without announcing it is designed to be photographed).

## Common culinary missteps to avoid

1. **Over-decorating plates.** The modern premium standard is restrained plating. Ten micro-garnishes on a plate reads as dated.
2. **One vegan dish as afterthought.** If 30% of guests are vegetarian, design vegetarian dishes with equal creativity, not as a fallback.
3. **Serving too early.** Food arriving before guests are settled creates chaos. Build buffer into the run-sheet.
4. **Dessert as an afterthought.** Dessert is the last taste guests have. It is disproportionately remembered.
5. **Ignoring the arrival drink.** Guests remember the first sip as much as the first sight. It is the palate handshake.

## When the event is specifically culinary (food-forward)

Some events (chef's table, product launch for a food brand, restaurant opening) make food the star. In those cases:
- The menu section becomes longer than other sections.
- Plating, technique, sourcing of ingredients are detailed.
- A chef's bio or philosophy may appear in the proposal.
- Beverage pairing is elevated to a protagonist, not a supporting role.

---

## What "done" looks like

A catering company receiving this menu document knows:
- How many people, what they're serving, in what style.
- Which dietary options exist and how many to prepare.
- What the brand heart means for presentation.
- What the signature drink is and its non-alcoholic version.

And the client reading the proposal feels the food before they eat it.
